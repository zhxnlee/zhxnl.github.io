Introduction

Cancer is a generic term for a large group of diseases that can affect any part of the body, and other terms used are malignant tumours and neoplams. In this page, we will be exploring more about the percentage of Cancer globally, the death rate of Cancer, the most prominent Cancer type and the main causes of the most prominent Cancer type.

What is the rank of Cancer in causes of deaths?

Figure 1 shows a lollipop chart that represents the Cause of Deaths from various diseases throughout the years. The x-axis represents the total number of deaths from each disease, and the y-axis represents the cause of deaths showing all the important diseases. A selection filter "Continent" can be used to filter the lollipop chart based on the desired continent, and a slider filter can be used to visualise the changes of the cause of deaths from each disease throughout the years.

According to the World Health Organization (2022) and the lollipop chart on the left taken from Our World in Data (Max Roser and Hannah Ritchie, 2015), Cancer is the second leading cause of death globally, accounting for an estimated 10 million deaths in 2019. The number of deaths for Cancer continues to grow from the year 1990 of about 5 million deaths to the year 2019 of about 10 milion deaths as we apply the slider filter. This is almost twice as much deaths in Cancer alone in 29 years of time!

In this page, we will be finding out more on the types of cancers, how much does it affect the global population, and its related causes.

What are the Risk Factors for Lung Cancer deaths?

As we have seen above that the number of deaths from "Tracheal, bronchus and lung cancer" is the highest among deaths of other cancer types, we now dive deeper into the risk factors of Lung Cancer. According to the National Library of Medicine of an article "Smoking and Lung Cancer" (Walser, T. et al, 2008), it is estimated that cigerette smoking explains almost 90% of lung cancer risk in men and 70% to 80% in women. It is stated that the smoking is the most important risk factor for lung cancer and the data supporting this relationship by Walser, T. et al (2018) are compelling.

Figure 6 on the left shows the different risk factors that causes lung cancer deaths. The x-axis represents the total number of deaths for each risk factors, and the y-axis represents the different risk factors throughout the years for a particular gender. The year can be filtered by using the "Year" filter slider, and the Gender can be selected by using the selection filter on "Gender" Figure 6 shows that the top risk factors (excluding behavioural, environmental risks) are Tobacco and Smoking, with a higher difference in values between them and the other values in Male, compared to Female.

Figure 7: Death rates of Smoking

Brief insight on Smoking

As we have seen from above that the risk factor for most deaths of Lung Cancer are from Tobacco and Smoking. We will now have a brief look at the death rate for Smoking.

Figure 7 shows a choropleth map of the death rate of smoking per 100,000 people throughout the years. A slider filter for the year can be used to visualise the deathrate globally from the year 1990 to 2019. The choropleth map can also be filtered based on the highest global percentage of a country with the highest death rate of smoking, and continents can also be chosen from the selection filter.

Based on Figure 7, it seems that Greenland and Myanmar has the highest percentage of Smoking compared to the other countries. As we apply the Year filter from 1990 to 2019, it is obvious that the death rate for Smoking has significantly decreased all around the world, where the saturation of the choropleth map decreases to a lower color value.

Conclusion

Cancer is the second leading deaths globally, with Lung Cancer topping the charts of the number of deaths for all cancer types, and Tobacco and Smoking is one of the highest leading factors to lung cancer.
